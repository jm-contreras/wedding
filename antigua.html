Visiting Antigua & Guatemala

Before You Go

Passport & Visa

Citizens of the US, Canada, UK, EU, Australia, and most Latin American countries do not need a visa for stays under 90 days — just a valid passport with at least six months of validity remaining from your date of entry. If you're from elsewhere, check your country's requirements with the Guatemalan consulate.

Vaccinations & Health Prep

No vaccinations are required to enter Guatemala from the US, Canada, or Europe. The CDC recommends being up to date on routine vaccines, plus considering Hepatitis A and Typhoid for stays involving food and water exposure. We're not staying in malaria zones — Antigua is at altitude and Atitlán is fine. Talk to your doctor or a travel clinic 4–6 weeks before the trip if you'd like personalized guidance.

Travel Insurance

We recommend it. Basic policies are inexpensive and cover medical, lost luggage, and trip interruption. World Nomads, SafetyWing, and Allianz all work fine for Guatemala.

Phone & Connectivity

Most US carriers (T-Mobile, Verizon, AT&T) offer Guatemala roaming — check your plan. eSIMs from Airalo or Holafly are cheap and easy if you'd rather skip roaming charges. WiFi is widely available in hotels, cafés, and restaurants in Antigua.

Weather

February is dry season — the best time of year to be there. Antigua sits at 1,500m (5,000ft); days are sunny and around 70°F (21°C), nights drop to around 50°F (10°C). The sun is strong despite the cool temperatures. Rain is very unlikely.

Plugs & Voltage

Guatemala uses Type A and Type B plugs (the same as the US and Canada) and 120V power. No adapter needed if you're traveling from North America.

Getting There

Flying In

Fly into La Aurora International Airport (GUA) in Guatemala City. Direct flights run from Miami, Houston, Dallas, Atlanta, Los Angeles, New York (JFK), Madrid, Mexico City, and several Central American hubs. From most US cities without direct service, you'll connect through Houston or Miami.

Immigration & Customs

Lines move quickly. You'll fill out a short customs form on the plane or at a kiosk. After immigration, your bags are X-rayed at customs (random check). The whole process usually takes 30–45 minutes.

Airport to Antigua

Antigua is about 1 hour from GUA — longer in Guatemala City rush hour (7–9am, 5–7pm). Three options:

  • Pre-booked shuttle — easiest. ~$15–25 USD per person, shared van, drops you at your hotel. Antigua Tours and Atitrans are reliable. Book 24+ hours ahead.
  • Uber — works at GUA. Expect ~$30–45 USD to Antigua. Confirm the driver before getting in.
  • Private transfer — most hotels arrange these. ~$50–80 USD for a private car. Worth it if you're arriving late or with a lot of luggage.

Avoid unmarked taxis at the airport. Taxis Amarillo (yellow taxis) are the licensed option if you didn't pre-book.

Getting Around Antigua

Antigua is small — central Antigua is roughly 8 by 8 blocks, and almost everything you'll want is walkable. The streets are cobblestone, so wear shoes you can walk in. The whole town is laid out on a grid: avenidas run north–south, calles east–west, numbered outward from Parque Central.

When You Need a Ride

  • Uber works in Antigua and is usually the easiest option. Cheap (~$2–4 USD across town).
  • Tuk-tuks (three-wheeled motor taxis) are everywhere. Negotiate the price first — Q10–25 within central Antigua is fair.
  • Taxis wait around Parque Central. Confirm the price before getting in.

Day Trips

For Pacaya, Acatenango, coffee fincas, and Lake Atitlán, book through your hotel or with operators like Old Town Outfitters, OX Expeditions, or Antigua Tours. Shuttle services to Atitlán run multiple times a day (~$15 each way, 3 hours).

Money & Tipping

Currency

The Guatemalan quetzal (GTQ, written as Q). Roughly Q7.7 to $1 USD as of writing. USD is widely accepted in Antigua, especially at hotels and tourist-facing businesses, but you'll get better value paying in quetzales.

Cards vs. Cash

Most hotels, restaurants, and larger shops in Antigua accept Visa and Mastercard. Amex is hit or miss. Bring some cash for tuk-tuks, markets, smaller cafés, tips, and tour operators that prefer cash. ATMs are easy to find around Parque Central — withdraw quetzales directly. Notify your bank of travel to avoid card freezes.

Tipping

  • Restaurants: 10% is standard. Some places auto-include it (look for servicio on the bill); if so, no need to add more.
  • Hotel staff: Q10–20 per bag for porters, Q20–40 per night for housekeeping if you stay multiple nights.
  • Tour guides & drivers: 10–15% of the tour cost is generous and appreciated.
  • Tuk-tuks & taxis: Tipping isn't expected — round up if you'd like.

Food & Water

Drinking Water

Drink bottled or filtered water — not tap. Hotels generally provide refillable bottled water in rooms. Most restaurants serve filtered water, but if you're unsure, ask: ¿El agua es purificada? Ice at established restaurants is usually fine; skip ice from street vendors. Brushing teeth with tap water is generally OK in Antigua but bottled is safer.

Street Food & Markets

Antigua has incredible street food. Use the same judgment you would anywhere: busy stands with high turnover are safer than empty ones. Cooked-to-order is safer than sitting food. The Mercado Municipal and the food stands at La Merced are legitimately good. If you have a sensitive stomach, ease in — start with cooked food and bottled drinks the first day.

What to Try

  • Pepián — Guatemala's national dish. A rich, smoky stew with chicken or beef.
  • Kak'ik — a Mayan turkey soup with cilantro and chiles.
  • Chiles rellenos — stuffed peppers, often battered and fried.
  • Tamales — wrapped in banana leaf rather than corn husk; richer than Mexican tamales.
  • Rellenitos — sweet plantain dumplings stuffed with refried beans. Trust us.
  • Coffee. Guatemala grows some of the best coffee in the world. Visit a finca, or just drink it everywhere you go.

Safety & Health

General Safety

Antigua is one of the safest cities in Central America and is very welcoming to visitors. Standard travel precautions apply: don't flash valuables, keep your phone in your front pocket, take Uber or licensed taxis at night rather than walking unfamiliar streets alone. The historic center is well-lit and patrolled.

Altitude

Antigua is at 1,500m (5,000ft). Most people don't notice it, but you may feel slightly more breathless on stairs the first day. Drink extra water and ease into alcohol your first night. Lake Atitlán is similar (1,560m). Acatenango summit is 3,976m — that's altitude that matters; the trek includes acclimatization.

Sun

At altitude, near the equator, the sun is much stronger than it feels. SPF 30+, reapply, and wear a hat — especially for anything outdoors during the day. Easy to underestimate.

Stomach Issues

Even careful travelers sometimes get a mild stomach upset adjusting to new food and water. Pack Imodium and Pepto, plus oral rehydration salts (Pedialyte packets). Most cases resolve in a day. Pharmacies in Antigua are well stocked if you need something.

Emergency Numbers

  • Police: 110
  • Tourist police (Antigua): +502 7965 4711
  • Fire / ambulance: 122 or 123

What to Pack

  • Comfortable walking shoes — cobblestones are no joke. Sneakers, low boots, or sturdy flats.
  • Festive cocktail outfit for the wedding day (think bohemian garden soirée). For women, a wrap dress or jumpsuit; for men, a linen suit or sharp shirt with trousers. Bring a layer for the evening.
  • Layers for cool evenings — a light jacket, sweater, or shawl. February nights are crisp.
  • Sunscreen, sunglasses, and a hat — strong sun at altitude.
  • Reusable water bottle — most hotels have refill stations.
  • Basic medical kit — Imodium, Pepto, ibuprofen, electrolyte packets, Band-Aids.
  • Cash & cards — bring some USD to exchange or use, plus your debit card for ATMs.
  • A small day bag for walking around — secure, not too flashy.
  • If you're hiking Acatenango or Pacaya: warm layers, gloves, hat, headlamp, broken-in hiking boots. Tour operators provide some gear, but check what's included.

Things to Do in Antigua

Wander

The best thing to do in Antigua is just walk. Cobblestone streets, pastel colonial houses, ruined churches around every corner, market stalls, courtyards behind unmarked doors. Don't over-plan — get coffee at one of the cafés around Parque Central and let the day go where it goes.

Cerro de la Cruz

A 20-minute walk uphill north of town to a viewpoint with the city spread below and Volcán Agua framing the horizon. Best at sunset. Go in a group or with a tourist police escort (free, available at Parque Central).

Coffee Tours

Filadelfia, De La Gente, and Finca El Pilar all offer half-day tours that walk you through bean to cup. Filadelfia is polished and resort-style; De La Gente is a small cooperative experience; El Pilar is closer to nature.

Volcanoes

Pacaya is a half-day hike (3–4 hours active, easy-moderate). Active volcano, dramatic lava fields, no overnight required.

Acatenango is a 2-day overnight trek. You camp on the side of the volcano and watch neighboring Volcán Fuego erupt through the night. Strenuous (3,976m summit), unforgettable. Book with a reputable operator (OX Expeditions, Tropicana, Wicho & Charlie's). Don't underestimate the cold and altitude.

Markets

Mercado de Artesanías near Parque Central is the easy option for textiles, woven goods, and souvenirs. The Mercado Municipal (next to it) is where locals shop — produce, prepared food, household goods. For serious textile shopping, take a day trip to Chichicastenango (Thursdays and Sundays) or San Antonio Aguas Calientes.

Ruins & Churches

The whole city is a UNESCO World Heritage site. Don't miss: Iglesia de la Merced (yellow façade, beautiful inside), Convento de las Capuchinas (the best-preserved ruin to walk through), Catedral de San José (the cathedral on Parque Central — the back ruins are open to visit), Convento de Santo Domingo (now a hotel and museum complex). And of course, Santa Clara — where we'll see you.

Beyond Antigua

If you're staying longer, Guatemala has more to offer than most countries five times its size.

Lake Atitlán

Three hours from Antigua. A volcanic crater lake ringed by villages and three more volcanoes. Stay in San Marcos (yoga, swimming, slow), Santa Cruz (boutique hotels, lakeside restaurants), or Panajachel (the main hub with the most options). Boats connect all the villages. Allow 2–4 days. We'll be there Feb 18–20.

Tikal

The most spectacular Mayan ruins in Central America, deep in the Petén jungle. Fly from Guatemala City to Flores (1 hour), then drive an hour to the park. Allow 2 days minimum, with at least one sunrise tour. Book lodging inside the park (Jungle Lodge, Tikal Inn) for the best access.

Semuc Champey

A series of turquoise pools cascading over a natural limestone bridge in the highlands. Remote — about 8 hours by road from Antigua. Worth it if you have the time and are up for adventure. Most people do it as a 2- or 3-day trip from Antigua via Lanquín.

Pacific Beaches

Black-sand surf beaches a couple hours from Antigua. El Paredón is the laid-back surf town; Monterrico is mellower, with sea turtle nesting. Good for a 1- or 2-night decompression.

Río Dulce & the Caribbean

Guatemala's Caribbean coast is small but distinctive — Garífuna culture, Livingston, and the river jungles around Río Dulce. Best reached by short flight to Puerto Barrios or via Tikal.
