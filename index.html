Travel

A short guide to getting to La Antigua and making the most of your time in Guatemala. For first-time visitors, see our full travel guide for more detail.

Getting to Guatemala

Fly into La Aurora International Airport (GUA) in Guatemala City. Direct flights run from Miami, Houston, Dallas, Atlanta, Los Angeles, New York, Madrid, and several Central American hubs.

Airport to Antigua

Antigua is about a one-hour drive from GUA. Pre-booked shuttles are the easiest option (~$15–25 USD per person). Uber works at the airport. Private transfers can be arranged through your hotel.

Where to Stay

We're working on a hotel block in central Antigua, walking distance from Santa Clara. Booking details will go out with the formal invitation. In the meantime, central Antigua near Parque Central is the right neighborhood to look in.

Things to Do in Antigua

Wander cobblestone streets, climb Cerro de la Cruz for sunset views, tour a coffee finca, hike Pacaya or Acatenango volcanoes, and explore the colonial ruins scattered across the city.

Before or After the Wedding

Guatemala rewards extra days. Lake Atitlán (3 hours from Antigua), the Mayan ruins of Tikal (a short flight to Flores), the turquoise pools of Semuc Champey, and Pacific or Caribbean beaches are all within reach. We'll be at Lake Atitlán the weekend before the wedding and traveling casually for a few days after — if you're around, ask us, you're welcome to join.

What's the dress code?

Festive cocktail — think bohemian garden soirée. Elevated but relaxed; the most beautiful dinner party, not a black-tie event. Antigua's evenings cool down quickly, so bring a light wrap or jacket. Cobblestone streets are not heel-friendly. The welcome buffet and farewell brunch are casual.

What kind of ceremony is it?

Spiritual, not religious. The ceremony is led by an aj'quij — a Mayan spiritual guide — with Aymara elements honoring Juanma's Bolivian heritage and a Celtic processional honoring Morgan's Irish lineage. Sacred but warm. A bilingual program will walk guests through the symbolism.

Will the wedding be bilingual?

Yes — the ceremony and key moments will weave together English and Spanish, so guests from both sides feel at home.

Are kids welcome?

We're still working out the details — final guidance will go out with the formal invitation. The whole week is built around family, and we want loved ones to feel they can turn the trip into a family vacation if they wish.

What about dietary restrictions?

We'll ask about dietary needs on the RSVP form — vegetarian, vegan, gluten-free, and allergy options will all be accommodated.
